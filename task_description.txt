Welcome to the Environmental Sound Classification Challenge!

Our world is full of sounds, from the chirping of birds in the morning to the blaring of sirens in the city. While humans can naturally identify and 
categorize these audio signals, teaching a machine to do the same is a fascinating challenge in the field of Artificial Intelligence.


🎯 Overview of the Task

In this competition, your task is to build a Machine Learning or Deep Learning model capable of classifying short,
 5-second audio clips into one of 50 distinct environmental categories.

You will be working with a modified version of the famous ESC-50 dataset.
 We have provided a training set (where the answers are known) and a test set (where you must predict the answers).

Your Target: A simple baseline model (k-Nearest Neighbors / Random Forest) has
 been provided in the starter materials. Your primary goal is to engineer better features, build a stronger model, and beat this baseline score!

🚀 Getting Started & GPU Resources

1. Start Simple (CPU) Before jumping into complex neural networks, 
we highly recommend experimenting first with traditional 
Machine Learning models using scikit-learn (just like the provided baseline). 
These models train very fast on a standard laptop CPU and are the best way to test out your initial feature engineering (like tweaking your MFCC parameters).

2. Scaling Up (GPU) Once you are ready to experiment with Deep Learning architectures (like running Convolutional Neural Networks on audio spectrograms), 
training on a standard CPU will become quite slow.

When you reach that point, you will need a GPU. 
While Kaggle provides some free GPU hours directly in their Notebooks, we also highly recommend using Google Colab as an alternative workspace. 
It gives you free access to powerful GPUs (like the NVIDIA T4). Simply upload the dataset to your Google Drive, mount it in your Colab notebook,
 and you are ready to train your advanced models!

Good luck, have fun, and let's see whose model has the best hearing!

Data & Evaluation

💾 The Data & Submission Format

You are provided with two main data archives:

train_audio.zip: Contains the audio files and a train.csv file with the correct labels for you to train your model.
test_audio.zip: Contains the audio files you need to evaluate.
To understand exactly how to submit your predictions to Kaggle, please look at the sample_submission.csv file. 
Your final submission must match this format exactly (two columns: filename and target).

📊 Evaluation Metric

Submissions are evaluated on Categorical Accuracy. This metric simply calculates the percentage of audio files in the test set for which your model predicted the exact correct category.

🕵️‍♂️ Public vs. Private Leaderboard

When you make a submission, you will immediately see your score on the Public Leaderboard. However, this score is calculated using only 50% of the test data.

The remaining 50% of the test data is kept secret and is used to calculate the Private Leaderboard. The final competition standings will be determined exclusively by the Private Leaderboard at the end of the competition. This system exists to ensure models actually learn the patterns of the audio, rather than just "overfitting" or memorizing the public test data.

(Note: You are allowed up to 5 submissions per day!)
